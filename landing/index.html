§ The problem

AI made building fast. Racing toward a fake signal is just being wrong faster.

Every loop of development now sprints toward its success signal. But speed toward a proxy that doesn't track reality — the Goodhart trap — just gets you confidently, expensively wrong.

The scarce thing is no longer build speed. It's whether the signal each loop chases is honest. That's the layer Oraculum guards.

§ Loop Engineering · it refuses to fake it

Same discipline, three sources of truth.

Loop Engineering is the craft of building across AI-native development's three loops — agentic coding, developer feedback, and product-market-fit. Oraculum is its honesty layer: it keeps each loop measuring something real.

One rule across every loop: judge where you honestly can, refuse where you can't, and hand the bet back to you. The line between the two is the whole product — and it runs through all three loops.

§ The method · fuzzy evaluation

Fuzzy targets are where evals fake it most.

Tone, quality, faithfulness, "on-brand" — the moment success is a matter of judgment, there is no checkable predicate. Oraculum draws the same line here: calibrate the judge, or don't trust it.

BLOCKED · FUZZY_JUDGE
The uncalibrated judge

An LLM scores "quality" or "tone" with nothing to check itself against. It looks like an eval; it's laundered opinion.

✕carries position, verbosity & self-preference bias
✕the metric climbs while quality doesn't
Oraculum blocks it by construction.
THE HONEST PATH
The calibrated judge

A human-labeled golden set, plus a required judge-vs-human agreement — Cohen's κ — before the judge is trusted at all.

✓measure κ against human labels before trusting it
✓re-check κ as the judge's own regression signal
Calibrate first, trust second.
◷ maturity Today Oraculum blocks uncalibrated fuzzy targets rather than fake them. The calibrated-judge runtime is the frontier it's built toward — the gate already recognizes the honest path and refuses everything short of it.

§ Intellectual lineage

Not a new theory. A rigorous assembly of what testing research already knows.

Three established literatures, one structural claim: you cannot trust an oracle you have not validated — and validation is statistical, not self-asserted.

01
The test-oracle problem

The Verdict Engine is a probabilistic test oracle in this survey's formal sense — defined, with soundness and completeness. Not a coined term; a defined one.

Barr, Harman, McMinn, Shahbaz, Yoo.
IEEE TSE 41(5), 2015.
02
Flaky-test quantification

Grounds the stress-run acceptance and the power bar that makes "ran it once, didn't see it" statistically invalid. A 1-in-300 flake won't show in 10 reruns.

Micco (Google, 2016);
Luo et al., FSE 2014; FlakeRate.
03
LLM-as-judge calibration

Grounds "calibrate the judge against a golden set before you trust it," and Cohen's κ as the judge's own regression signal. Uncalibrated judges carry systematic bias.

Gu et al. survey, 2024;
Zheng et al., NeurIPS 2023.
